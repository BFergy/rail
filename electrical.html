DESTINATIONS





 

Electrifying Thoughts

Many, if not most, modellers detest the job of wiring the layout. And I get that. It can be a real chore working under the layout. It can also be a challenge to remember what all the various wires actually do. And, of course, we would all prefer to spend the time that wiring takes on other things like running trains, building structures, and doing scenery. But, it is a necessary evil. My approach has always been to treat it like just another aspect of the hobby, and to do it in an organized, logical fashion. I suppose I'm in the minority, since electronics is a second hobby for me, but I really don't mind doing wiring. Although, at my age, working under the layout often gives me pain in parts of my body I forgot existed. Oh well...

I use DCC on my layout so that will always be my focus on everything I talk about below. But similar principles apply for DC layouts.

I was determined to avoid any soldering beneath the layout and I have managed to meet that goal. Terminal blocks/strips and ferrules made life much easier. Every wire connection made from below was done using ferrules. Simply strip the wire, insert it into the ferrule, crimp, and secure it in the terminal location. Ferrules are about $0.02 each on Amazon and a ferrule crimper can be had for $20-30. I use them for wiring in many unrelated applications too.

 

Power Handling versus Wire Choice

Choosing suitable gauges of wire for different functions may seem like a black art but it all comes down to how much power a given wire will ever need to handle. Common DCC systems have a maximum output of 2 to 10 amps. Large layouts may employ a power booster but again these usually are 10 amp or less. The wiring that feeds this power around the layout is the 'DCC bus'. Many layouts also have a separate, secondary DC supply for purposes such as structure lighting, switch machines, crossing signals, etc. This wiring is the 'accessory bus'. It would be rare for this bus to ever handle more than a few amps in total.

Some layouts are divided into multiple 'power districts'. Each district is fed separately from the base power supply or a booster. This prevents a problem in one district from affecting the rest of the layout. Each district, even if there is only one, should have an electronic circuit breaker to protect the DCC base power station. Regardless, the DCC bus in a district will never need to handle more current than the base unit or booster can provide.

What all this means is that you do not need a DCC bus made from wire capable of handling 40 amps on a 5 amp system, unless your layout is the size of a warehouse. I have seen instances where peope have used 12-gauge or even 10-gauge wire - this is major overkill, expensive, difficult to work with, and totally unnecessary.

I have a 5-amp NCE PowerPro wireless system which is perfect for me. My DCC bus is 18-gauge, stranded wire (sometimes referred to as lamp cord). Depending on which source you reference, it has a rating of 10 to 12 amps, with no measureable voltage drop on a layout my size. More than adequate for my needs and quite inexpensive. Mine is a two-wire, siamesed pair, with a red and a black wire, making polarization easy. I got a 100-foot roll from Amazon for about $16. I used the same wire for my 12V accessory bus.

I chose to use 22-gauge solid wire for my track power feeders. I dropped power feed wires from every section of track except for turnouts and some very short filler pieces. Thus, the small wire is adequate because there are multiple wires delivering power to the rails at the same time. Even on their own, each feed point can handle up to 2 amps and I will never have more than 1 or 2 locomotives on any one piece of track.

Several years ago, I built some high-current, multi-output DC power supplies using surplus PC supplies that I had laying around. While definitely overkill, I had a spare one so I used it. An inline, 2-amp fuse protects the layout wiring. I have no accessories that require high current and used stranded wire ranging from 20 to 24-gauge for everything.

 

Welcome to the Machine

Apologies for the above Pink Floyd reference...

I have 11 turnouts on my shelf layout. Early on, I decided to employ switch machines on all of them, in part because a few would require reaching over structures or scenery, but also because I'm a bit geeky about simple electronics and automation. I wanted quiet, slow-motion machines rather than the noisier snap-action type. I had no interest in using DCC to operate them - just simple panel control.

Back in 2024, while researching the various units on the market, I stumbled upon a YouTube podcast episode of TSG Multimedia where John Abatecola interviewed Seth Neumann of Model Railroad Control Systems. MRCS, in California, had begun importing the line of MTB switch machines from Europe. I was fascinated by their tiny size, their simplicity, and also their low cost. The total range of motion is adjustable to account for different scales - they are preset for HO.

The basic MP1 model had the exact features I wanted so I ordered 11 of them. Including shipping, the bill was a hair under $200 US - very acceptable for 11 machines. They were on backorder at the time but mine shipped after about 2 weeks and I had them shortly thereafter.

 

 

I tested all of them upon arrival and they all worked perfectly.

Before running bus wires or doing any other work under the layout, I needed to know the exact position of each switch machine so I could avoid any conflicts. The easiest solution was to actually install them!

The first step was to center the turnout points. Two small strips of card stock, folded in half, did the job. The hole in the throwbar was thus in the center of the turnout's movement range.

 

 

The actuator rod that comes with the MTB MP1 switch machine is .044" diameter and is quite stiff. I wanted a bit more spring in the rod so I cut mine from .032" music wire instead, which the MP1 easily accomodates (this is also recommended by MRCS). I made mine a bit longer to make it easier to install the machine.

I needed the machine to be in the center of its throw. This is easily done by briefly applying 12V to the power terminals until centered.

I attached the actuator rod to the machine and headed under the layout. I fed the rod up through the hole in the throwbar and aligned the face of the machine with the throwbar so motion would be in the proper directions. When satisfied with the alignment, I held the machine in place and used a pencil to mark the mounting slots on the plywood. I removed the machine and drilled a very small and short pilot hole in the center of the marked slot for the 2.5mm screws that I used. I put the machine back in place and snugged it up with the screws, being careful not to cause any damage by overtightening.

I attached temporary wires to the MP1 power terminals and, from above, applied power and made sure the turnout was throwing correctly in both directions. If not, I made minor adjustments with the mounting screws or the slider and repeated the process until satisfied. These machines were very east to install.

 

 

With all the switch machines in place, it was time to get wired!

 

On The Bus

Obviously, the bus wiring starts from the source. I had already decided on a corner of the room where I would locate all the control equipment. So that was where I started installing both the DCC bus and also the accessory bus. I installed a 2-row terminal block under the layout, leaving adequate space for an NCE EB1 circuit breaker and a fuse for the accessory bus. The DCC track feeds, DCC programming feeds, and the accessory bus wires would connect here.

I had an old, unused stereo cabinet from prehistoric times that had been collecting dust for many years so I resurrected it for my control electronics and power supplies. The aforementioned feeds connect from here to the underside of the shelf. I left plenty of slack in those cables to allow the cabinet to be rolled out for any needed work in that corner.

 

 

I decided to run the DCC bus toward the rear of the shelf and the accessory bus toward the front. The bus wires adhere to a polarity scheme of red to the front of the layout and black to the back. As the bus wires progressed around the layout I inserted breakout boards in the buses to enable easy connections. These breakout boards (shown below) allow for solderless connections and are something I made for myself and, for a while, also offered them for sale on my former Imageworkz page. The green boards handle up to 8 amps continuous and the blue ones handle up to 15 amps continuous. I use the green for my 12V accessory bus and the blue for DCC.

 

 

Track Power Feeders

Power to the track is delivered by a total of 27 pairs of feeder wires from the DCC bus to the rails above. 22-gauge, solid wire is used for the feeders. To account for polarity, I used one red and one black wire, with red to the front and black to the back. I drilled holes for these from above, adjacent to the rails, between ties, for almost every piece of track except turnouts and extremely short pieces. I used a #51 drill bit (.067") which accomodated the wire I used. I stripped about 1/4" of the wire, bent the last 3/32" or so at 90 , and soldered that to the bottom flange of the rail on its outside edge. I didn't take photos during this process but you can see a feeder in the pic below (taken after I had started airbrushing the track).

 

 

After the track is ballasted, the feeders will be virtually invisible. Underneath, the feeder wires were run to a suitable DCC breakout board location, a ferrule was crimped on, and they were connected to the bus.

 

Wiring Organization

Messy wiring is a pet peeve of mine. A typical layout has an enormous number of wires beneath it. If these are run haphazardly, the end result can look like a rat's nest. Tracing problems can be a nightmare, as can expansion or modification to a particular circuit. Personally, I treat wiring like any other aspect of the hobby and try to do the best job I can.

 

 

And yes, I buy P-clamps and cable ties by the bag from Amazon!

I use a separate drawing layer in AnyRail to map and number the locations of track feeder points, switch machines, terminal blocks, breakout boards, etc. I also have a spreadsheet where every wire between those objects is identified by its source, destination, colour, and function if appropriate, right down to the specific terminal(s) it connects to. In effect, this is the equivalent of a gigantic circuit schematic diagram. Is this overkill? Perhaps, but I like the peace of mind it gives me. For example, if I ever need to know where the wire goes from pin 6 on the back of terminal block TB2, I can look it up, and find out it runs to pin 5 on switch machine T7. Hopefully, I will rarely need to use this information but I have it handy.

 

Switches Switching Switches

With 11 machine-operated turnouts, I had chosen to use two control panels. One panel handling the 8 turnouts in and adjacent to the Southgate yard, and one panel for the 3 turnouts on the Saugeen spur.

The MP1 machines have 6 wire connection points. They use 3 wires for motion control - a +12V common power feed and 2 wires for direction control (whichever one is grounded determines direction of throw). They can also switch an auxilliary common connection to 1 of 2 other connections (again depending on direction) - these can be used as desired by the user. I connected the +12V common power feed directly to my 12V bus. I use the machine's auxilliary contacts to indicate turnout status on my control panels via LEDs. I jumper the main MP1 common to the auxilliary common with an 825K-ohm resistor (so my panels do not need resistors in them for the LEDs). Bottom line is that each switch machine connects to the panel with 4 wires.

We found some 22-gauge, 2-wire, stranded, jacketed cable at a discount store for under 10 cents per foot. I bought 200 feet but probably got 250. I used well under half of it to run cables from the 11 switch machines to their relevant control panel area. There, these cables terminate in dual-row terminal blocks mounted near the back of the shelf (refer to photo above).

I wanted the panels to be easily removeable from the layout. With so many wires involved I decided to use IDC ribbon cables and sockets for the MP1 connections and terminal blocks for the other wires. I designed simple circuit boards for this and sent the files off to be made into bare boards. Partly assembled ones shown below.

 

 

The board on the left mounts to the back of the Southgate panel. The board on the right mounts under the shelf. The two boards are identical and serve both purposes. All soldering was, of course, done at the workbench. I also designed the board so one could be cut in half and the two halves used for the smaller Saugeen panel. The ribbon cables and sockets came from Amazon. The terminal blocks are the same ones used on my breakout boards.

The control panels are simple boxes, with a 12mm plywood frame, 5mm plywood back, and an inset face panel. I was going to use 1/8" polycarbonate, painted on the inside, for the face panels, but...while wandering around Princess Auto one day, came across some 5" x 12" carbon fibre panels that were 1/8" thick. One piece would make both of my panels so I grabbed one. At $12 it wasn't cheap but I love the look of carbon fibre. What I don't love, however, is machining it... I killed a few saw blades and drill bits, and that work proceeded slowly.

I mounted the toggle switches and 3mm LEDs to the front panel, and then began the lengthy job of soldering all the wires in place between the front and rear panels. I guess the best term for the result would be 'organized chaos'...

 

 

Assembly of the control panel box is a simple case of passsing the circuit board through the rear and screwing it in place, then attaching the front panel.

I mounted the finished unit under the front rail of the shelf at a slight upward-facing angle.

 

 

The toggles for the turnouts are SPDT units that connect the appropriate wire from the MP1 to ground. I used 3mm LEDs - green for the primary route and red for the secondary. The toggle labelled 'TRK' and 'PRG' is a DPDT, center off switch for the isolated shop track,allowing it to be connected to the main DCC bus, used as the programming track, or disconnected entirely. The white graphics are just self-adhesive vinyl made with my PC and vinyl cutter.

 

Structure Lighting

Whenever I add buildings to the layout, I add interior and/or exterior lighting. I use either 0402 or 0602 LEDs for this - those microscopic things that are a pain to work with. I 3D-print my own light housings and fixtures for these. I add current limiting resistors to all of them. I tie them together inside the building and drop a single set of power connections to a suitable location under the layout. I connect those to a small terminal block so that the building can be removed from the layout easily for other work.

From the terminal block, I connect the negative side to the ground of the 12V accessory bus. To faciltate turning the lighting on and off, and also to provide dimmer control, I run the positive lead from the terminal block to a switch and potentiometer housed in a bracket that I 3D-printed.

 

 

In the above pic, the right side of a latching push-button switch is connected to +12 on the accessory bus. The left side of the switch is jumpered to the right tab on the potentiometer. The center tab is connected to the positive side of the lighting terminal block for the building. The left tab is unused.

The bracket mounts to the back of the shelf front rail, near the building, and just high enough to be out of the way.
