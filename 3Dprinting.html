To Print or Not to Print?

Do modellers need to go down the rabbit hole of 3D printing? Absolutely NOT! There are countless producers of 3D-printed detail items, structures, and even rolling stock. You can find almost anything you want, and the number of offerings is growing almost daily.

There is also the expense involved in doing your own 3D printing - not just in terms of the equipment and necessary consumables but also the time involved in learning how to use the printer and create your own printable designs. The latter can literally eat up weeks of your time, possibly even a few months, learning how to use 3D CAD software.

The above isn't meant to deter anyone from entering the world of 3D printing but, rather, just to point out that it is far more complex than many people realize, and many, if not most, modellers simply don't need to even consider it.

However, if you have room in your life for another hobby, which, in essence it is, 3D printing can be incredibly valuable both in the train room and outside of it.

 

My Journey

I got into 3D printing back in very early 2021, before I even got serious about designing and building my HO layout. Both myself, and my son, have a diverse range of interests, and, I like to think, are somewhat imaginative and creative. We constantly found ourselves wishing for simple things that either weren't available, weren't exactly what we wanted, or were very expensive.

One day, while searching Amazon for something unrelated, a 3D printer popped up in the results. I made the mistake(?) of taking a quick look. For days after, I researched brands and models, watched countless YouTube videos, and began to see the possibilities. Shortly thereafter, I ordered an Elegoo Mars 2 Pro (the latest and greatest at the time) and a bottle of resin. Having successfully printed the test file that comes with the printer on a USB stick, I fell completely into the rabbit hole.

An unused corner of my laundry room became 3D headquarters. After cleaning, resin prints need to be post-cured under UV light, so I built a curing box. A search for 3D CAD software, preferably free, led me to a site called OnShape (I use their free on-line version to this day). Learning their software, at a basic level, took me about a month but at that point I could produce printable files for most of the things I wanted to make. I've come to grips with some of OnShape's more advanced functions since then. I'm certainly not a design expert but, so far, I've been able to create everything I've wanted.

Many people replace or upgrade their equipment as the new machines hit the market but I'm still using my original machine (5 years old as of this writing) and it just keeps humming along. You can see my simple setup below.

 

 

Practical Applications

I didn't realize the number of things I would do with a 3D printer until I had one. Perhaps the strangest was a situation where the toilet paper roll holder in my bathroom broke at the end when I dropped it while changing the roll. Instead of replacing it at the local hardware store for about $10, I popped out the broken end cap, 3D printed a replacement for under 15 cents, and glued it in. It's still 'rolling' along after 5 years!

I've printed numerous cordless tool battery holders, tool holders, circuit board standoffs, small project cases, automotive gauge mounts,...the list is endless. If you can dream it up and draw it, and it fits within the printer's size limits, and you have an appropriate resin, you can make it!

 

Modelling Applications

By now, you are already aware of a plethora of 3D-printed items available within our hobby. If someone already produces what you want/need, I encourage you to support them. Even if you have a printer, it will save you time and effort, and help ensure they continue to develop and produce new items.

For me, I started designing and making my own detail parts before they were readily available on the market. Indeed, before even starting my layout! Oil drums, jersey barriers, garbage cans, dumpsters, bollards, roof details, building vents, etc., etc. I have a good stock of items waiting to find homes on my layout.

I've also designed several things that just aren't on the market - they haven't been produced yet, or size/style don't suit me, etc.

Considerations, Operation, & Consumables

There are two basic types of 3D printers - resin printers and filament printers. The filament variety are great for creating strong, practical items but lack the ability to reproduce fine details. Resin printers are basically the opposite (though some resins also have considerable inherent strrength). For our purposes, the resin printer is the clear winner.

Obviously, you need a suitable location to set up and use the printer. The kitchen, living area, or bedroom is not that location! Neither is an unheated garage, cold basement, or anywhere that airborne dust is an issue. A temperature controlled (preferably 21°C or higher), relatively isolated, clean area is best. For me, I have a space about 2 feet wide and 8 feet long in my laundry room that has been quite adequate for me. The printer needs a perfectly level surface on which to sit. In the first photo, top of page, you can see how I created a work top that sits on an existing cabinet - a piece of 5/8" MDF with laminate on top, 2 pieces of 1 1/4" square aluminum tube screwed beneath that, with 4 threaded levelling legs. A wall-mounted shelf holds my UV curing cabinet and a few other items. My printer and curing cabinet are both plugged into a surge-protected power bar with an on/off switch. Printers don't like to be bumped or shaken during use, so I have a great excuse for not doing laundry when I'm printing!

Uncured resin can be mildly toxic to some people and pets. It has never affected me but I've seen cases where people have developed a severe rash after just slight contact. Nitrile gloves offer very good protection. Obviously, children should never be allowed near the printer or cleaning supplies.

There are numerous brands and dozens of types of resin on the market. Each has a chemical formulation that suits specific purposes. My brand of choice is Siraya Tech. For mechanical or utilitarian objects where strength and/or machinability (drilling, tapping, etc.) are needed, I use their 'Build' resin. For detail parts, their 'Fast Tough Grey' is my go-to. In my experience, good resin isn't cheap and cheap resin isn't good.

Printed items need to be cleaned prior to final post-curing. There are wash-and-cure stations available now but not when I started and I continue to do it all manually. Regardless of method, a cleaning fluid is required. The most common is 99% isopropyl alcohol (IPA) although I switched to methyl hydrate as I found it cleaned better and cost far less. Both, however, are extremely flammable, so that must be considered. And both, like resin, also have a strong odour. Some form of ventilation (using a brushless fan!) might need to be considered.

After a print cycle is complete, I let the machine sit for at least 30 minutes, allowing excess resin to drip back into the vat. I use 3 large plastic vats with self-sealing, snap-on lids (dollar store items), nearly filled with methyl hydrate. I carefully remove the build plate (printed items still attached) from the printer, holding it over paper towel, and then rinse everything in the tubs, moving sequentially from tub 1 to tub 3, for about 30-60 seconds in each. Then I lay the build plate on its side on a bed of paper towel on a hard plastic tray. In about an hour, everything is dry enough to handle and the printed items can be removed from the build plate. I give the build plate a wipe down with paper towel dampened with cleaner, and return it to the machine. Notice how many times I've said 'paper towel' - keep plenty on hand in the work area. Transfer soiled towelling to an open garbage can or bag until thoroughly dry.

Please do NOT allow resin or cleaner to enter sink drains!

I give printed parts at least 2 or 3 hours before post-curing them in the UV cabinet (below).

 

 

The inside of the cabinet measures 8"W x 8"D x 11"H. On top is a 60W UV lamp in the correct wavelength for resin curing. The box is lined with a thick, silver, self-adhesive vinyl film. The turntable is UV-powered.

Curing time depends on many things - size, thickness, type of resin, etc. and is best determined through experience. After curing, items can be primed and painted.

Some people drain the resin vat after printing, filtering it back into the resin container. Unless I'm changing to a different resin, I don't. I leave it in the vat, and next time I print, I stir it thoroughly until well mixed. For this, I use a small, soft paint brush about 1/2" wide. I clean the brush in my cleaning vats. I add additional resin if needed.

The printer's resin vat has clear film in the bottom, commonly made from FEP (Fluorinated Ethylene Propylene). It allows UV light to pass through for print exposure. It is a consumable and must occasionally be changed, especially if it becomes worn or is damaged by a failed print. Using a damaged film can result in resin leakage and that can really ruin your day! I've never had a leak (yet) and have changed the film only 2 or 3 times in 5 years but I always have at least one spare on hand. Changing it is time consuming but not overly difficult - the key is getting the tension of the new film right.

 

In Summary

Taking a dive into the depths of 3D printing is not a wise choice for everyone. There is a commitment of money, time, and also space. For those not already versed in 3D design software, the learning curve can be quite steep. 3D printers are not simply 'plug-n-play'.

But...if everything said on this page doesn't scare you away, 3D printing opens up an entirely new world of creative possibilities, both within the hobby and also outside of it. Personally, I'm glad I took the plunge. Even though I only average about one print per week, and sometimes go a month or so without touching it, my printer has become a tool that I rely on.
